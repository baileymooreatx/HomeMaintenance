# Insulation

## What Is R-Value?

The **R-value** is a measure of **thermal resistance**, indicating how well a
material resists the conductive flow of heat. The "R" stands for **resistance**.
In the context of building and construction, a **higher R-value** means the
material is a **better insulator**, reducing heat loss in winter and heat gain
in summer.

Mathematically, R-value is defined as the temperature difference per unit of
heat flux.

$$
R_{\text{val}} = \frac{\Delta T}{\phi_q}
$$

Where $$R_{\text{val}}$$ is the R-value (in $$K \cdot m^2 / W$$  or 
$$^\circ F \cdot ft^2 \cdot h / BTU$$), $$\Delta T$$ is the temperature 
difference between the two sides of the material, and $$\phi_q$$ is the heat 
flux (heat flow per unit area)

## Units and Measurement

R-values can be expressed in two primary unit systems:

- **I-P (Inch-Pound) units**: Commonly used in the United States.
- **SI (Metric) units**: Often referred to as **RSI-value**.

An R-value in I-P units is approximately **5.68 times larger** than its
equivalent in SI units. For example, an R-2 window in I-P units has an RSI of
roughly 0.35.

## How R-Values Work

### Additivity

R-values are generally **additive** for multiple layers of dense solid materials
in direct contact. For instance, if you have two layers of insulation with
R-values of R-10 and R-15, the total R-value of the assembly is R-25.

### Diminishing Returns

While adding insulation always improves performance, the benefit follows the law
of **diminishing returns**. Doubling the R-value halves the heat loss, but the
cost to add that extra insulation may eventually outweigh the energy savings.
For example, going from no insulation to R-10 saves significantly more energy
than going from R-30 to R-40.

## Typical R-Values by Material

Different materials offer different levels of resistance per inch of thickness.
Here are some common examples:

| Material                   | R-Value per Inch (Approx.) |
|:---------------------------|:---------------------------|
| **Closed Cell Spray Foam** | R-7.0                      |
| **Open Cell Spray Foam**   | R-3.8                      |
| **Fiberglass (Batt)**      | R-3.1 – R-4.3              |
| **Mineral Wool**           | R-3.7                      |
| **Foam Board**             | R-4.0                      |
| **Wood (Softwood)**        | R-1.41                     |
| **Concrete**               | R-0.1 – R-0.2              |
| **Brick (Common)**         | R-0.2                      |

## Recommended R-Values for Homes

The ideal R-value depends on your **climate zone** and the part of the house
being insulated. The U.S. Department of Energy and ENERGY STAR provide specific
recommendations:

### Attics

Attics require the highest levels of insulation because heat rises.

- **Warm Climates (Zones 1-2):** R-30 to R-49
- **Mixed Climates (Zones 3-5):** R-38 to R-60
- **Cold Climates (Zones 6-7):** R-49 to R-60

### Walls

- **2x4 Walls:** Typically accommodate R-13 to R-15.
- **2x6 Walls:** Typically accommodate R-19 to R-21.

### Floors

- **General:** Minimum R-13.
- **Colder Climates:** Recommendations increase to R-25 or R-30 for floors above
  unheated spaces.

## Important Considerations

While R-value is the standard metric, it primarily measures resistance to
**conductive** heat flow under steady-state conditions. It does not fully 
account for:

- **Air Leakage:** Insulation like spray foam can act as an air barrier, which
  fiberglass batts do not, potentially making them more effective in practice
  even at similar R-values.
- **Installation Quality:** Gaps, compression, or moisture can significantly
  reduce the effective R-value of installed insulation.
- **Radiant and Convective Heat:** In some low-density materials, these factors
  can influence performance beyond the standard R-value rating.
